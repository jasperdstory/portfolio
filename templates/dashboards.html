A desktop app for retail teams, to monitor, share, and act on business intelligence. How we could bring context to decisions in every team by co-building with our users.

Bringing context to all decisions for higher performing teams.

(2 min read)

Before Dashboards, the main way users accessed Viewsy was through email reports. Users loved that they received a summary of key store statistics and analysis sent to their inbox every week or month. Accessing reports was frictionless, and they looked great in mobile mailboxes.

But reports weren’t flexible enough to fit into the complex structure of a modern retail business. Different retail teams have different priorities. There were teams in place to manage each of the hundred or more stores. Marketing and merchandising teams at head office launch sophisticated campaigns. A layer of business leaders at the top set the strategic course for the company across many channels. Different teams teams had different time-horizons: for store team the last week was important, for executives the last six months.

So while email reports were a great start, they weren’t targeted, focused or relevant enough for different teams in different retailers. But we were resistant to content-creation tools (letting users build the reports themselves), because it meant user experience and build complexity.

User experience complexity was a big problem for even best-in-class business intelligence products for organisations, products like Tableau or Domo. They were too complex and too general. Users want to know their critical results. They don’t want to construct bar graphs, move about generic data objects, and tweak axes. The industry leaders had sacrificed simplicity to support the many industries they targeted.

Our users were left to piece together information from different data stores, spreadsheets, PDFs and emails. But they didn’t have the time to learn a complex user interface or reason about business data models, so typically decisions were made without any data to support them.

Enter Dashboards

To minimise build complexity we used open source libraries for key functionality, and looked closely at our existing data models and how we could manage and minimise builds on the data flow. To maximise manageability and build agility, we built with React.

To minimise user complexity, we had to thoughtfully plan the user experience. We wanted to deliver an experience that delivered the right information for each user and team. The user would do no heavy lifting.

Right for every team

Dashboards were designed around “cards”. Every Dashboard contained cards showing a particular result or visualisation. Careful design ensured that cards were laid out simply and clearly on all screen sizes.

Simplicity through relevance

By targeting retail teams specifically, our cards could be structured around retail use-cases. An example are leaderboards, which rank store results. For an area manager or exec, these tables can provide a simple, quick overview of their estate. In a typical BI tool, users would create a table, then for each row set as an “object”, filtering for stores, set each column as a “dimensions” filtering for statistic types, set aggregation types and format, add labels, and then more selections to get things working. With our “Leaderboards” card, users would drag-and-drop some stores and drag-and-drop some KPIs. That’s it.

Prioritise what matters to you

Easy sharing was a pre-requisite for a system like this, but this would mean users had a range of Dashboards available to them. Within every team, every user has different priorities. An area manager, for example, might be. With simple favouriting, users would have their critical dashboards available to them in the top-left of their screen; the most important screen estate on the page.
